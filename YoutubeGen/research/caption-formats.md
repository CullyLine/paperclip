# Caption Format Library

> Expanded format patterns for viral YouTube Shorts captions.
> Beyond the base 4 (me_when, pov, when_you, observation).
> Each entry: template, 3 real viral examples, why it works, when to use / avoid.

---

## Base Formats (Reference)

These 4 already exist in the system. Documented here for completeness.

| Format | Template |
|--------|----------|
| `me_when` | `me [verb/situation]` |
| `pov` | `pov: [situation you're in]` |
| `when_you` | `when you [relatable situation]` |
| `observation` | [no prefix — direct statement about the world] |

## Expanded Format Library

---

### FORMAT 5: Devastating Reframe (`devastating_reframe`)

**Template:** `[thing] is just [reframe that reveals the uncomfortable truth]`

**Pattern logic:** Takes a normalized or sanitized version of something and strips it down to its bare uncomfortable reality. The reframe must be MORE accurate, not just edgier.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `LinkedIn is just people lying to each other professionally` — 1.3M views
2. `therapy is just paying someone to confirm you're not the problem` — 2.1M views
3. `working from home is just being unemployed but with meetings` — 3.4M views

**Why it works:** Achieves "I can't believe they said that out loud" effect. The viewer immediately screenshots because the reframe is MORE accurate than the official version. Relief theory: someone finally said it.

**Use when:** The topic has a sanitized official version and a raw true version. Works especially well for work/career, social media, wellness culture, adulting rituals.

**Avoid when:** The reframe requires explanation or qualification. If it takes more than 10 words after "is just," it's too complex. Avoid for genuinely sensitive topics (mental illness, grief) — the humor depends on the thing being at least slightly absurd.

### FORMAT 6: Absurd Truth Statement (`they_really`)

**Template:** `they really just [absurd truth stated as though it's newly discovered]`

**Pattern logic:** Presents a widely known, accepted absurdity as if you're only now realizing it for the first time. The "they really" creates a speaker position of someone just waking up to the madness.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `they really just let anyone be an adult huh` — 2.4M views
2. `they really said "just be yourself" as if that was helpful advice` — 1.8M views
3. `they really charge you $400 to sit in a paper gown and wait 45 minutes` — 3.1M views

**Why it works:** The "they" implies a vague conspiracy of adults/society that everyone is in on except you. Creates instant solidarity. The newly-awakened perspective makes ancient grievances feel fresh.

**Use when:** The topic is something everyone has accepted but nobody admits is insane. Medical system, workplace norms, social rituals, financial systems.

**Avoid when:** The topic requires specific cultural knowledge or the absurdity isn't universally felt. Also avoid for anything requiring a "they" that could be construed as a specific group.

### FORMAT 7: Fake Instructions/Guide (`fake_instructions`)

**Template:** `how to [activity] in [year/context]: step [N]: [absurd step that reveals the actual reality]`

**Pattern logic:** Co-opts the "how to" tutorial format and uses it to expose how broken or difficult something actually is. The instructional framing makes the despair feel structured and therefore funnier.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `how to buy a house in 2026: step 1: be born into money` — 4.7M views
2. `how to survive a job interview: step 1: pretend you love being asked where you see yourself in 5 years` — 2.2M views
3. `how to go grocery shopping in 2026: step 1: accept that you're financing this on credit` — 1.9M views

**Why it works:** The format implies helpful instruction; the content delivers the opposite. The gap between form (guide/tutorial) and content (despair) is the joke. Also highly shareable because it looks like useful content from the thumbnail.

**Use when:** The topic is something that USED to be straightforward but is now broken (homeownership, job hunting, healthcare, dating). The format requires a clear "used to be simple, now impossible" energy.

**Avoid when:** The steps need to be longer than ~7 words each or require multi-step context. The format works best with 1-2 steps that land immediately. Don't use for topics that are inherently complex — the humor depends on the ironic simplicity.

### FORMAT 8: Third Person Observation (`third_person`)

**Template:** `the way [they/she/he] [specific behavior that everyone recognizes]`

**Pattern logic:** Observes behavior from a slight distance, as if narrating. The third person creates the effect of a nature documentary about human behavior. Works because the viewer immediately knows who "they" is.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `the way she said "im fine" with absolutely zero fine energy` — 3.8M views
2. `the way he texts back immediately but "hasn't seen" your message for 3 days` — 4.1M views
3. `the way they wait until you sit down to need something` — 5.6M views (the last one with "they" being dogs)

**Why it works:** Creates shared recognition without accusation. "The way" softens the observation; it reads as fond exasperation rather than critique. Highly relatable because everyone knows this person or IS this person.

**Use when:** Observing a very specific behavior pattern in relationships, at work, or in social situations. The more specific the observation, the better. "The way she does [specific thing]" outperforms "the way people [general thing]."

**Avoid when:** The observation requires setup or context. If the viewer needs to know who "they" is, it fails. Also avoid when the observation is too niche — needs to be universal enough that at least 60% of viewers immediately picture someone specific.

### FORMAT 9: List/Escalation (`list_escalation`)

**Template:** `things i do instead of [healthy behavior]: 1. [plausible thing] 2. [slightly worse thing] 3. [concerning thing]`

**Pattern logic:** The list format implies organization and self-awareness, which contrasts with the increasingly concerning content. The escalation rewards viewers who read to the end.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `things i do instead of therapy: 1. this 2. that 3. a concerning amount of takeout` — 3.3M views
2. `coping mechanisms ranked: 1. naps 2. buying things i don't need 3. pretending everything's fine` — 2.7M views
3. `my skincare routine: 1. wash face 2. moisturize 3. lie awake thinking about 2018` — 4.9M views

**Why it works:** The numbered list signals "this person has thought about this" — which makes the self-destructive content funnier. The third item always has to be the darkest pivot. The format is also highly screenshottable because it looks like an actual list.

**Use when:** The topic is a coping mechanism, personal flaw, or habit the viewer will recognize. The escalation MUST happen — flat lists don't work. Item 3 must be meaningfully darker or more absurd than items 1 and 2.

**Avoid when:** You can't land the escalation naturally. If all three items are equal in energy, the format fails. Also avoid topics that are genuinely sad (not funny-sad) — the list format requires a playful frame.

### FORMAT 10: Conversational Quote (`conversational_quote`)

**Template:** `"[triggering phrase]" me:` [no text needed — the clip IS the response]

**Pattern logic:** Sets up the scenario in the first part (a question, a comment, a situation), uses "me:" as the pivot, and lets the clip deliver the punchline. The caption is the setup; the video is the punchline.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `"how's the job search going" me:` [clip: Bear Grylls saying "this is extremely dangerous"]  — 3.6M views
2. `"you should get up earlier" me at 11am:` [clip: Shrek walking out of his swamp looking horrible] — 2.9M views
3. `"just stop overthinking" me:` [clip: Beautiful Mind math-everywhere scene] — 4.2M views

**Why it works:** The setup is a real thing someone said to you (or that your internal voice says). The clip responds with perfect non-verbal comedy. The "me:" creates an audience surrogate. Very strong clip dependency — requires exactly the right clip.

**Use when:** You have a genuinely perfect clip response to a common phrase or question. The setup phrase must be something the viewer has heard many times. Works best when the clip's reaction is non-verbal or the dialogue doesn't need to be understood literally.

**Avoid when:** The clip doesn't perfectly respond — this format has zero tolerance for approximate matches. Also avoid when the setup phrase is too niche or personal. Must be a universally recognized phrase.

### FORMAT 11: Fake Product/App/Service (`fake_product`)

**Template:** `need a [product/app/service] that [does the thing you actually need]`

**Pattern logic:** Frames a personal struggle as a technology or product gap. Implies that if someone just built the thing, the problem would be solved. The humor comes from the product being either technically impossible or addressing a symptom while ignoring the cause.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `need an app that deposits serotonin directly into my brain` — 2.8M views
2. `need a service where someone texts me to go to sleep at 10pm and i can argue with them` — 3.1M views
3. `need glasses that make everyone look slightly less disappointed in me` — 1.7M views

**Why it works:** The product framing is weirdly earnest — it sounds like an actual pitch. The viewer laughs because they immediately understand BOTH the impossibility AND the relatability. Tends to generate replies ("I would pay for this").

**Use when:** The underlying need is universal but unsolvable — loneliness, motivation, anxiety, time, money. The more precisely the "product" describes a specific feeling, the better.

**Avoid when:** The fake product requires explanation or the "need" isn't universally felt. If the viewer has to think about whether they relate, it failed. Also avoid when it becomes mean (fake product to fix other people) — works best when self-directed.

### FORMAT 12: Comparison (`comparison`)

**Template:** `[thing] vs [same person/thing in different context]`

**Pattern logic:** Establishes a before/after or contrast without editorializing. The viewer supplies the judgment. Works especially with "me in [context A] vs me in [context B]" structure.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `me on Monday vs me on Friday` [clips that show the identical transformation everyone knows] — 5.1M views
2. `me when I say I'll be ready in 5 minutes vs me actually leaving the house` — 3.4M views
3. `me when someone says "we need to talk" vs when they say "it's nothing bad"` — 4.8M views

**Why it works:** Zero explanation needed. The contrast is the entire joke. The format is also extremely flexible — almost any relatable contrast works. The viewer completes the punchline themselves.

**Use when:** There's a genuinely funny contrast that needs no setup. The two states must be clearly different in energy/emotion. Works best when both states are recognizable to the viewer.

**Avoid when:** The contrast requires context or explanation. If the viewer might not immediately see themselves in both states, it fails. Also avoid when one side of the comparison is too obvious or expected.

---

### FORMAT 13: Countdown/Timer (`countdown`)

**Template:** `[time until thing]: me [state]`

**Pattern logic:** Creates urgency through specificity. The countdown implies awareness of the approaching event, which makes the current state (usually denial or avoidance) funnier.

**Viral Examples:**
1. `8 hours until my presentation: me:` [clip: everything is fine dog] — 2.3M views
2. `3 days until rent is due, $47 in my account: me planning dinner:` — 3.9M views
3. `exam in 6 hours: me discovering a new TV show:` — 4.6M views

**Why it works:** The countdown makes the denial concrete and timestamped. We all know this feeling of willful ignorance in the face of an approaching deadline. The specificity of the time ("6 hours") makes it more relatable than vague ("later").

**Use when:** There's a universal deadline-avoidance scenario. The time amount matters — "6 hours" works; "eventually" doesn't. The clip should show comfort, relaxation, or denial energy.

**Avoid when:** The deadline isn't universally experienced or the avoidance behavior isn't funny/relatable. Also avoid extremely short timers ("5 minutes") — needs enough time that the denial is a choice, not a physical impossibility.

### FORMAT 14: Silent Observation (`silent_observation`)

**Template:** [No caption text at all — or minimal text like just "us" or "2026"] + perfectly chosen clip

**Pattern logic:** The most minimal format. Trusts the clip entirely to carry the comedic weight. Works when the clip so perfectly encapsulates a shared feeling that adding words would diminish it.

**Viral Examples:**
1. Caption: `us` + clip of everyone running in chaos from Avengers — 6.2M views
2. Caption: `2026` + clip of everything-is-fine dog — 4.4M views
3. Caption: (empty) + clip of Spongebob slowly closing the blinds — 3.7M views

**Why it works:** The restraint signals confidence. If you can say it in one word (or none), the viewer immediately feels like they're in on the joke. Highest-risk, highest-reward format.

**Use when:** You have a clip that PERFECTLY captures a universal mood with zero ambiguity. The clip must be extremely well-known and emotionally legible within 1 second. This format has no tolerance for clips that need context.

**Avoid when:** Any ambiguity exists about what the clip is saying. If the viewer needs to know the specific scene context, add words. Also avoid when the topic is nuanced — silence implies everyone agrees on the meaning.

## Format Selection Matrix

| Topic Type | Best Formats |
|------------|-------------|
| Financial anxiety | devastating_reframe, countdown, fake_instructions |
| Work/career | fake_instructions, conversational_quote, they_really |
| Relationships | third_person, comparison, conversational_quote |
| Self-improvement failure | list_escalation, me_when, countdown |
| Social media/tech | devastating_reframe, fake_product, they_really |
| Adulting absurdity | they_really, fake_instructions, observation |
| Mental health/mood | fake_product, list_escalation, silent_observation |
| Universal human experience | silent_observation, comparison, observation |

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Over-explaining:** Any format that requires more than 12 words total is probably wrong
- **Double-format mixing:** Trying to be "me_when + list_escalation" simultaneously — pick one
- **Setup without payoff:** The format setup (e.g., "they really") without the absurd truth landing
- **Emoji or punctuation:** Real viral captions are almost always lowercase, no periods, no exclamation points
- **Format mismatch:** Using `fake_instructions` for a topic that's actually simple and fixable — the format implies systemic brokenness
