# Zoomer Humor Caption Analysis

Research for the YouTube Shorts Generator Pipeline — analyzing the "black bar caption + movie clip" format, identifying patterns, and collecting high-performing examples.

---

## Format Anatomy

The viral format consists of:

```
┌─────────────────────────┐
│                         │
│   BLACK BAR (~25%)      │
│   White text caption    │
│   centered, 2-4 lines  │
│                         │
├─────────────────────────┤
│                         │
│                         │
│   MOVIE / TV CLIP       │
│   (~75% of frame)       │
│   Reaction moment       │
│                         │
│                         │
│                         │
└─────────────────────────┘
```

- **Resolution**: 1080×1920 (9:16 vertical)
- **Caption area**: Top ~25% of frame, solid black background
- **Text**: White, sans-serif (typically bold), centered horizontally and vertically within the black bar
- **Text size**: Large enough to read on mobile, auto-scaled to fit — typically 2-4 lines max
- **Clip**: The bottom ~75%, cropped/letterboxed from the original film aspect ratio

## What Makes These Captions Work

### Core Psychological Hooks

1. **Instant relatability** — the viewer's first reaction should be "literally me"
2. **Emotional specificity** — the more specific the scenario, the more universal it feels
3. **Tension between caption and clip** — the movie clip "reacts" to the caption, creating a punchline
4. **No explanation needed** — if you have to explain it, it's not funny
5. **First-person framing** — "me when" / "POV" / "when you" creates identification

### Tone Rules

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Self-deprecating | Self-pitying |
| Absurdist but grounded | Random for the sake of random |
| Cynical but not bitter | Genuinely mean-spirited |
| Observational with a twist | Obvious observations |
| Post-ironic (sincere through irony) | Pure sincerity or pure irony |
| Reference shared generational experiences | Reference niche/insider knowledge |
| Acknowledge existential dread casually | Be actually depressing |
| Hyperbolic reactions to mundane things | Underreact to genuinely serious things |

### Structural Patterns

1. **Setup → punchline in the specificity**: Start general, end with an absurdly specific detail
2. **Escalation**: List things that get progressively worse/more absurd
3. **Contrast**: Pair a serious framing with a trivial subject (or vice versa)
4. **Time compression**: "for the 47th time" / "every single day" / "since 2019"
5. **False confidence**: Present a clearly bad situation as if it's fine

## Caption Categories & Examples

### Category 1: "Me When..." (Highest Volume)

The workhorse format. Simple, direct, endlessly adaptable.

1. "me watching my savings account after paying rent, student loans, and buying a single grocery"
2. "me explaining to my boss why I need a mental health day after receiving a mildly passive-aggressive email"
3. "me pretending I didn't see my coworker at the grocery store so I don't have to make small talk on my day off"
4. "me calculating how many hours of overtime I need to afford doing literally anything fun this month"
5. "me after saying 'we should hang out soon' knowing full well neither of us will ever follow up"
6. "me googling my symptoms at 3am and accepting that I have a rare disease found only in 18th century sailors"
7. "me opening my laptop for 'just one quick email' and somehow it's 2am and I've reorganized my entire digital life"
8. "me listening to my friend's relationship drama for the 4th time this week about the same person they keep going back to"

### Category 2: "POV:" (Immersive/Second-Person)

Puts the viewer directly into the scenario.

9. "POV: you just explained your job to your parents for the 47th time and they still think you 'do something with computers'"
10. "POV: you're the friend who always has to pick the restaurant because everyone else 'doesn't care' but will reject every suggestion"
11. "POV: you accidentally opened the front camera and had to confront reality"
12. "POV: you said 'I'm on my way' but you haven't even found your keys yet"
13. "POV: you're pretending to read the menu even though you already know exactly what you're going to order"
14. "POV: you've been 'about to start working out' for 3 consecutive years"
15. "POV: the group chat went silent after you sent a meme and now you're questioning your entire sense of humor"

### Category 3: "When You..." (Universal Observer)

Slightly more detached, observational.

16. "when you realize 'the adults' you were waiting to show up and handle things was supposed to be you this whole time"
17. "when the 'quick 5-minute task' at work has now consumed your entire afternoon and possibly your will to live"
18. "when you rehearse an entire argument in the shower and absolutely destroy them but then in person you just say 'yeah no that's fine'"
19. "when someone asks what you did over the weekend and you have to make 'absolutely nothing' sound like a lifestyle choice"
20. "when you check your bank account after a weekend of 'treating yourself' and the number has a personal vendetta against you"
21. "when your phone dies at 23% because it too has given up"
22. "when you set 7 alarms and still manage to be late like it's a competitive sport"

### Category 4: Observational / No Prefix

Direct statements, often absurdist or existential.

23. "nobody talks about the walk back to your car after a job interview where you definitely did not get the job"
24. "that one friend who says 'I'll be there in 10' from a location that is physically 45 minutes away"
25. "the way I can memorize every lyric to a song I heard once in 2014 but can't remember why I walked into a room"
26. "adults really just pick a dinner and eat it every week for the rest of their lives and that's completely normal apparently"
27. "nothing humbles you faster than seeing yourself in a store security camera"
28. "the gym is a great place to go if you want to feel confident for 45 minutes and then eat everything in your fridge when you get home"
29. "telling yourself 'I'll remember this, I don't need to write it down' is the most dangerous game a human can play"
30. "sleep schedule really said 'I'm a suggestion, not a rule'"

### Category 5: Current Events / Trending Tie-ins

These age faster but perform extremely well when timely.

31. "me checking if the economy recovered yet like I'm refreshing a tracking number"
32. "everybody's a financial advisor until their own portfolio looks like a horror movie"
33. "the way we all collectively decided to just not think about [trending crisis] and move on like nothing happened"
34. "me watching the housing market like I'm ever going to be a participant and not just a spectator"
35. "inflation really said 'everything is a luxury now, including existing'"

## Emotional Tone Rankings (by Performance)

Based on engagement patterns in the format:

| Rank | Tone | Why It Works | Example Vibe |
|------|------|-------------|--------------|
| 1 | **Relatable exhaustion** | Universal, low barrier | "me after existing for 8 hours straight" |
| 2 | **Self-deprecating** | Disarming, creates solidarity | "POV: you thought you had your life together" |
| 3 | **Absurdist** | Unexpected, shareable | "my last brain cell during a meeting" |
| 4 | **Nihilistic-lite** | Resonates deeply but feels safe | "nothing matters but also I need a snack" |
| 5 | **Observational irony** | Smart without being pretentious | "adults just pick a show and fall asleep to it forever" |
| 6 | **Nostalgic contrast** | Generational bonding | "being a kid vs being an adult" setups |
| 7 | **Current events cynicism** | Timely, high share rate | Economy/housing/dating app commentary |

## Caption Construction Rules for LLM Prompting

### Length Guidelines

- **Ideal**: 15-30 words
- **Maximum**: ~50 words (past this, text gets too small on mobile)
- **Lines**: 2-4 lines when rendered in the black bar
- **Max 5 words per line** for readability

### Grammar & Style

- Lowercase everything (no capitals except proper nouns)
- No periods at the end
- Minimal punctuation — commas and quotation marks only when needed
- Use "you" and "me/my" — never "one" or "a person"
- Numbers > words ("47th time" not "forty-seventh time")
- Contractions always ("don't" not "do not")
- Present tense or present participle ("me watching" not "me who watched")

### Red Flags (Captions That Fail)

- Too long / too many words (text becomes unreadable)
- Too vague ("when things are bad" — needs specificity)
- Trying too hard to be funny (forced wordplay, puns)
- Actually offensive vs. edgy-but-safe
- Boomer humor transplanted into zoomer format
- Corporate/brand voice leaking through
- Explaining the joke within the caption
- Referencing something too niche (inside jokes that exclude)

## Clip-Caption Pairing Principles

The movie/TV clip must function as a **visual punchline** or **emotional amplifier**:

| Caption Emotion | Ideal Clip Emotion | Example |
|----------------|-------------------|---------|
| Exhausted acceptance | Dead-inside stare | Office Space stapler guy |
| Pretending it's fine | Forced smile / mask | Harold (Hide the Pain) energy |
| Realization/horror | Dramatic zoom or face drop | Anakin/Padme "right?" meme |
| Defiant confidence | Smug walk or power move | Leo DiCaprio walking meme |
| Complete defeat | Collapse, head in hands | Any "I give up" movie moment |
| Confused panic | Frantic looking around | John Travolta Pulp Fiction |
| Quiet despair | Staring into distance | Ryan Gosling Blade Runner |

The clip should match the **emotional register** of the caption, not illustrate it literally. The humor comes from the juxtaposition of a mundane/relatable caption with a dramatic movie reaction.
